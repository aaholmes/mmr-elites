\begin{abstract}
Quality-Diversity (QD) algorithms discover diverse collections of high-performing solutions, enabling robust repertoires for robotics, creative design, and optimization.
The dominant approach, MAP-Elites, discretizes the behavior space into a grid and maintains one elite per cell---but this fails catastrophically when the behavior descriptor dimension exceeds 5, as exponential cell growth makes coverage impossible.
We introduce \mmr, which reformulates archive maintenance as Maximum Marginal Relevance (MMR) selection from information retrieval.
Rather than binning solutions into cells, \mmr scores each candidate by a weighted combination of fitness and minimum distance to the current archive, then greedily selects the top-$K$.
A lazy evaluation strategy with staleness tracking achieves $O(K \log K)$ expected selection complexity.
On an $N$-DOF planar arm benchmark, \mmr achieves $12\times$ better uniformity than \map at 20D (coefficient of variation $0.066$ vs.\ $0.832$) and $6\times$ better than \cvt ($0.417$), while maintaining comparable fitness.
\mmr scales gracefully to 100D behavior spaces where grid-based methods are infeasible.
The algorithm is distance-agnostic---requiring only a dissimilarity function, not explicit dimensions---opening QD optimization to behavior spaces with non-Euclidean structure.
Code is available at \url{https://github.com/aaholmes/mmr-elites}.
\end{abstract}
